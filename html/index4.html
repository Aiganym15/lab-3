An American Tragedy

An American Tragedy is a novel of naturalism presenting the story of a man struggling against social, economic, and environmental forcesóas well as forces within himselfóthat slowly drown him in a tide of misfortune. It was published in 1925 and became Dreiserís most successful novel.

Hamlet

“Hamlet” is one of William Shakespeare’s most famous and enduring tragedies. It tells the story of Prince Hamlet of Denmark, who is deeply affected by the sudden death of his father, King Hamlet. The play opens with a ghostly apparition claiming to be the spirit of King Hamlet, who reveals that he was murdered by his brother, Claudius, who has since become the new king by marrying Hamlet’s mother, Queen Gertrude.

Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy

The Hitchhiker’s Guide to the Galaxy explores and satirizes many facets of modern life, such as the legitimacy of authority, the absurdity of bureaucracy, and the search for the ultimate answer to the question of life, the universe, and everything. The book follows the galactic exploits of Arthur Dent, the last surviving Earthman, and Ford Prefect, originally of Betelgeuse, who escape the Earth immediately before its destruction at the hands of the Vogons.

Jenny Gerhardt

Theodore Dreiser's Jennie Gerhardt (1911) follows the plight of a socially disadvantaged woman whose love affairs with wealthy men put her in stressful situations, but enable her to survive all the same. Jennie is a victim and critics define her as "weak-willed," but the story reveals that every character is a victim of circumstance in some way: Lester to the expectations of an affluent society, Jennie to her romantic sensibilities, William and his family to their immigrant status and poverty.

Life, the Universe and Everything

In Life, the Universe and Everything, the third title in Douglas Adams' blockbusting sci-fi comedy series, The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy, Arthur Dent finds himself enlisted to prevent a galactic war. This edition includes exclusive bonus material from the Douglas Adams archives, and an introduction by Simon Brett, producer of the original radio broadcast.

Murder in the Rue Morgue

"The Murders in the Rue Morgue" is a short story by Edgar Allan Poe published in Graham's Magazine in 1841. It has been described as the first modern detective story; Poe referred to it as one of his "tales of ratiocination". C. Auguste Dupin is a man in Paris who solves the mystery of the brutal murder of two women.

Murder on the Orient Express

Murder on the Orient Express, first published in 1934, is a detective story by Agatha Christie featuring one of her most famous characters, the Belgian detective Hercule Poirot. The mystery of the locked room, the whole plot unfolds on the train, the Orient Express, which is stuck in a snowstorm. Poirot happens to be on a train when a man named Mr. Ratchett is killed. Poirot is called upon to solve this case, and the book tells about his investigation.

Night Light

Horror stories with nocturnal ghosts will keep you awake at night with stories about creatures from deep underground coal mines in the Appalachian Mountains, ghostly phenomena, mysterious prayer circles, a pact with the devil and the blood-curdling screams of the Jersey devil chasing a couple in the middle of the night in the pine barrens of New Jersey. New stories of suspense and horror from authors from the Mid-Atlantic region Ellen Perry Lewis, S.F. Varney, Charles Matthew, Virginia Parrish and Sammy Caramela are presented.

Othello

One of the most celebrated and widely performed tragedies of Shakespeare encompassing the feelings of love, hatred, deceit, and social ills prevalent in the Elizabethan society and the present world without any marked change, Othello was written in 1603 and first performed in 1604. The Tragedy of Othello explores the questions of racial prejudice and jealousy. The story talks about Othello, a black general suspecting his wife, Desdemona.
